In small groups, look at a real ad from any of these regions. Ask:
  1. What cultural value or belief is this ad using to persuade?
  2. What is being hidden or exaggerated?
  3. How would this same trick look in another part of the world?
  4. Is there any urgency created for the product which does not change for a long time?
  5. Are you experiencing the Fear Of Missing Out (FOMO)?
  6. Are they displaying the experts recommendations without the details of the experts?
  7. Are you provided with the social proof (like rated 5 stars by thousands)?
  8. Are there any exaggerated claims? (Like lose 10 kg in 2 weeks)
  9. Are they comparing the prices of the same prices over time? (like was $99, now $29)
  10. Are there any time boundaries or limitations for the purchase?
  11. Is there any complex/ jargon or technical terms to impress or confuse the customers?
  12. Are the retailers questioning your self image? (like be the first, be the smartest)
Each group should share their findings. This exercise reveals that while deceptive marketing wears different clothes in different cultures, the core strategy remains the same everywhere.
Deceptive marketing thrives because it adapts to its audience. It undermines consumer trust, distorts informed decision-making, and perpetuates inequality across all regions. Combating these practices requires strict legal enforcement, global awareness campaigns, ethical business practices, and an educated, vigilant consumer base. Knowing how it works in different regions not only makes us smarter consumers but also prepares us to recognize the same tricks in new forms. The best defense against deception is awareness.
