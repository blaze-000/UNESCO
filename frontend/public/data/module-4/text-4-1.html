RED FLAGS: Quick Signs of a Deceptive Ad
1. Watch the fine print
Ads often highlight the good stuff and hide the bad in tiny text, asterisks, or vague language. For example, smartphone ad touting "Free Screen Replacement" for a year, but in tiny text it states the free replacement is only valid for one specific minor crack, excludes battery and water damage, and requires customers to pay shipping fees and a costly diagnostic charge, making the "free" offer largely misleading.

2. Pictures and promises don’t match.
If the product you get looks nothing like the ad, that’s false advertising. A flashy burger on a billboard that turns out tiny and flat in real life? Classic case.

3. “Free” isn’t really free.
If you see “Free trial” or “Buy one, get one free,” check what strings are attached—like hidden shipping costs or automatic subscriptions.
4. Too urgent, too good, too vague.
If an ad screams “Only today!” or “Best deal ever!” without clear details, there you have to be careful. Companies create fake urgency to push you into quick decisions. A $500 phone “slashed” to $199 might sound amazing—but if it’s always been $199, that’s not a deal, it’s a Trick.
Example
Triangle Media Corporation deceptively advertised "risk-free" trials where consumers paid only a small shipping fee but were secretly charged full price ($98.71) and enrolled in costly subscriptions without clear consent, leading to a $9 million FTC settlement.
